Pinterest knows what you like.

You saved a Tarkovsky still. Now your feed is nothing but film references. You clicked on brutalist architecture once. Now it's concrete forever.

The algorithm is doing its job. Showing you more of what you already know you like.

But that's not how visual culture develops.

## The Algorithm Trap

You don't build a personal canon by consuming infinite variations on a theme. You build it by accidents. By stumbling across the thing you didn't know you were looking for. By connecting ideas that don't obviously belong together — Burial's album art to a Superstudio drawing to a Bourdieu quote.

**Pinterest optimizes for engagement. You need a tool that optimizes for discovery.**

## What Happens on Pinterest

You find something interesting. You save it. The algorithm shows you 50 more just like it. You save a few. Your feed becomes a monoculture. Everything looks the same.

Six months later, you have 2,000 pins. You can't find the one that actually mattered. The algorithm buried it under 1,800 variations that weren't quite right.

**Your collection grows. Your taste stagnates.**

## What Happens With a Real Visual Archive

You find something interesting. You save it. No algorithm takes over. No feed to scroll. Just your collection, waiting for you to return.

You don't see 50 variations. You see what you chose. You remember why you chose it.

Six months later, you find that reference in 10 seconds. You connect it to something new you discovered. Your canon evolves because your collection stays coherent.

## The Difference: Curation vs. Consumption

Pinterest is built for consuming infinite content. Infinite scroll. Infinite recommendations. Infinite variations of the same thing.

A real visual research tool is built for curation. Choosing what matters. Finding it again. Connecting it to new ideas.

**Pinterest asks: What do you want to see next?**  
**A real archive asks: What do you want to remember?**

## The Numbers Don't Lie

**Three years of Pinterest:**
- 10,000+ pins
- You remember 5 of them
- You can find 1 when you need it

**Three years with a personal archive:**
- 1,000 references
- You know why you saved each one
- You can find any of them in 10 seconds
- They've connected in ways that shaped your practice

## Who This Is For

Not everyone. If you want endless inspiration and don't care about finding things again, Pinterest is perfect.

But if you're building something — if your references need to talk to each other, if your taste needs to evolve, if you need to find that specific thing from 2022 — Pinterest will let you down.

**You need a tool that respects curation.**
